# Characters

## Character Philosophy

The central cast of *Purity Wars* should function on two levels at once:

1. as emotionally legible people with intimate loyalties, wounds, ambitions, and contradictions
2. as embodiments of the kingdom's ideological fracture around purity, identity, and social order

No major character should feel like a flat spokesperson for a political position. Even the clearest moral and thematic roles should remain grounded in love, fear, pride, grief, duty, or hope.

## Primary Cast

### King Aldren of Azzentia

- **Role:** King of Azzentia, central antagonist, defender of class purity
- **Function in the story:** the human face of the purity doctrine. He is not a tyrant by reputation. He is regarded by most of his people as a careful, reasonable, devoted ruler. He believes the kingdom would tear itself apart without the order he upholds, and he is willing to be the man who pays the personal cost of holding that order.
- **Core tension:** loves his daughter; cannot countenance what she has become. Public conviction and private grief sit in the same body.

### The Queen

- **Role:** Queen consort of Azzentia, moral hinge of the royal family
- **Function in the story:** stands between crown and child. She is intelligent, politically skilled, and emotionally exhausted. Her hesitation is not weakness — it is the only honest place a person can stand when the two things they love most are about to collide.
- **Core tension:** has known what her daughter is for longer than her husband. Has not chosen.

### The Princess

- **Role:** Heir to Azzentia, central protagonist of the larger ideological conflict
- **Function in the story:** the living contradiction the doctrine cannot survive. She did not choose to be multiclass to make a point — she *is* multiclass, and at some point she stops being able to pretend otherwise.
- **Core tension:** is young, in love with her own life, suddenly carrying a movement she did not ask to lead. Her courage is real, and so is her fear.

## Player Character

The player begins the game as a young figure close enough to the court to witness the royal split from inside. They are not yet a hero. They have a position, loyalties, small ambitions, and the kind of stake in everything-being-fine that makes the prologue's ending hit. Full identity choices (Pure or Mixed, class, narrative posture) come at the post-prologue character-creation scene.

See `06_player_narrative_design.md` for player-arc design.

## Supporting Cast Principles

Use side characters to embody how ideology lives in institutions: army, temple, trade, labour, scholarship, and rural life. Specifically:

- Each major institution that touches the war should have at least one named character whose private life is shaped by their public role.
- "Civilian witnesses" — innkeepers, parents, merchants, farmers — should not become disposable. The cost of the war is registered through them.
- Multiclass NPCs should not all be heroic. Some are bitter, some opportunistic, some quietly devoted to old loves they were forbidden to combine.
- Pure-faction NPCs should not all be cruel. Some are devout, some are afraid, some are simply inheritors of a worldview they did not invent.

## Writing Principles For Future Work

### The King

Do not write him as cartoonishly evil. He should be frightening because he is disciplined, loved by many, and convinced of his righteousness.

### The Queen

Do not write her as merely passive or kind. Her hesitation, intelligence, and eventual choice should matter politically.

### The Princess

Do not write her as a flawless progressive symbol. She should remain young, personal, and vulnerable even as she becomes mythic.

### The Player

Do not treat the player as a blank slate. The protagonist should have emotional stakes, social position, and believable tension before the player makes major choices.

### Supporting Cast

Use side characters to embody how ideology lives in institutions: army, temple, trade, labour, scholarship, and rural life.
