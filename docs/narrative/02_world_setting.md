# World Setting

## World Overview

**Ulcantron** is the wider world in which *Tales from Ulcantron* takes place. It is a high fantasy world of large continental landmasses, border islands, sacred territories, enchanted wilds, and culturally distinct kingdoms. Magik is woven into daily life — present, useful, regulated, and not quite mundane. Many cultures vary widely in tradition, politics, and temperament, but most of the known world shares a common understanding of *class* as the central organising principle of personal and civic identity.

Ulcantron is made up primarily of two large continents:

- **Bragaldita** — the leftmost of the two continents.
- **Zortinus** — the rightmost.

Smaller bordering islands exist between and around them. Magic kingdoms, sacred groves, enchanted forests, holy depths, and other "set-piece" fantasy biomes coexist with mundane terrain. The technology and aesthetic register sits between *Final Fantasy*-era JRPG and *Dungeons & Dragons*: swords and magik are the dominant tools, though some marksman-style cultures field early firearms.

## Azzentia

**Azzentia** is the kingdom in which *Purity Wars* takes place. It sits in the bottom-right of Bragaldita, semi-landlocked, with a rocky coast on its outward edge and farmlands, mills, medieval townships, and a few inland cities filling the rest. The capital is centrally placed; the kingdom is shaped roughly like an eight-way pizza slice radiating out from it. Each "slice" is a distinct region with its own civic flavour, dominant trades, and dominant class culture.

To outsiders, Azzentia is known as one of the most regal, conservative, and historically rigid kingdoms in Ulcantron — a place where institutions outlive the people who run them, and where tradition is less a habit than a duty. To insiders, it is also the place where multiclassing first became visibly common, which is exactly the contradiction *Purity Wars* turns on.

## Class as a World Rule

Class is the most important thing about a person in Azzentia. It functions simultaneously as:

- a profession
- a social role
- an inherited path
- a spiritual calling
- a magical or martial affinity

A person's class shapes nearly everything about how the kingdom treats them: their education, expected behaviour, marriage prospects, status, labour, civic duty, inheritance, social trust, and spiritual legitimacy. A person without a class — or with an unstable class identity — is often regarded as incomplete, suspect, or spiritually unmoored.

### Pure, Specialised, and Multiclass

- **Pure** — A single chosen class, e.g. *Knight*, *Mage*, *Bard*, *Rogue*. This is the social default and the doctrinal expectation.
- **Specialised** — A pure class deepened into a focused branch, e.g. *Holy Knight*, *Enchanted Mage*. Specialisation is celebrated; it is read as devotion.
- **Multiclass** — Two or more classes carried together, e.g. *Rogue Spellcaster*, *Bard/Thief*. Doctrinally, this is regarded as dilution — of the person, the institutions they touch, and (in the strongest reading) of the kingdom itself.

This vocabulary matters in-game because it is the same vocabulary used by characters, institutions, propaganda, and the player's own systemic choices.

## The Rite of Naming

In Azzentia, adolescents pass through a formal state-sanctioned process known as **The Rite of Naming**, a coming-of-age ritual in which class identity is recognised, affirmed, and publicly bound. The Rite typically occurs in the mid-teen years and is overseen jointly by doctrinal tutors, ritual officiants, and representatives of the relevant civic institutions. It is neither purely chosen nor entirely assigned: aptitude, lineage, calling, and personal preference are all weighed, and the result is recorded in civic ledgers.

The Rite is the legal moment when a young person becomes a *full participant* in Azzentian society. It is also the most common site at which the kingdom's purity doctrine is taught, enacted, and enforced — which is exactly what makes it a useful first-act setting for the game.

## Purity Doctrine

The official Azzentian position holds that class purity is what keeps the kingdom whole. Purity is taught by temples, civic schools, knight orders, the magical academies, and the crown itself. It is presented as:

- spiritually correct (each soul has *its* class)
- socially stabilising (clear roles, clear duties, clear status)
- aesthetically beautiful (mastery deepens; dilution muddies)
- safe (the things people fear about magik and arms are presumed to come from "mixed" hands)

The doctrine is genuinely attractive to many ordinary people — not only zealots. That attraction is part of why the war is hard.

## Multiclass Status

Multiclassing is not yet illegal in the prologue era, but it is socially marked. A multiclass adult can expect quieter doors, slower trust, and the constant low-grade pressure of being read as a problem. In some civic contexts (court appointments, knight orders, certain temple roles) multiclass identity is a disqualifying factor. The pressure to "pick one" is everywhere; some hide a second class their whole lives.

The princess refusing to hide is the moment that ends the kingdom's polite consensus.

## Magik

Magik (spelled with a *k* in-world) is woven into daily life: lighting, irrigation, farming aids, healing, civic record-keeping, communication, the more spectacular military and ceremonial use, and innumerable small workings. It is not marvelled at as a rarity, but it is not practiced by everyone — most people interact with magik the way modern people interact with electricity: pervasive, useful, *trusted to specialists*.
