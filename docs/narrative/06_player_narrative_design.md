# Player Narrative Design

## Status

**Stub.** Reserved for the player-character arc, the choice architecture across the campaign, the role of identity selection, and how the Pure / Mixed alignment becomes the systemic spine of the game. High-level player framing is in `01_narrative_overview.md`.

## What This Doc Will Contain

- **Player position at game start.** A young figure close enough to the court to witness the royal split from inside — court-attached attendant role in the prologue, full character creation immediately afterward.
- **Character creation.** What the player picks (class family, Pure vs. Mixed posture, presented gender, name) and what the engine then does with those choices systemically.
- **Pure vs. Mixed mechanics.** How the alignment is enforced in moment-to-moment play (allies who will or will not travel with the player, dialogue access, civic interactions, certain quests being closed or opened, certain abilities being unlocked or locked).
- **Choice architecture.** Where and how often the player makes meaningful narrative choices, and the difference between "flavor" choices (consequences scoped to a scene) and "axis" choices (consequences carried into the post-war epilogue).
- **The unwinnable beats.** The deliberate moments where the player loses, and why those are good for the story (see prologue for the model — `docs/prologue/01_prologue_implementation_plan.md`).
- **Companion design philosophy.** How party members reflect the alignment system without the game becoming a recruitment chase.

## Authoring Rules When Filled In

- Personalisation in the prologue stays light. Don't promise systemic depth there that you have to backfill in the character-creation scene.
- Choice points should always be readable as choices. If the player can't tell that they made a decision, the system isn't working.
- Pure-route and Mixed-route gameplay should be roughly equal in surface area. Neither is the "real" route.
- The player should be allowed to feel naïve, foolish, conflicted, or wrong without the game punishing them with a fail-state. Tragedy is not failure.
